Introduction

Rapid transit called "light-rail transit" (LRT) uses electric-powered single cars or brief trains to go over fixed rails. Not the physical weight of the cars, but the passenger capacity is what is meant by "light." LRT systems of today are based on conventional streetcar systems, but they have been rebuilt to travel farther and faster through urban areas.

LRT vehicles can operate similarly to streetcars on streets with at-grade junctions or in the medians of highways. LRT typically travels along completely dedicated right-of-ways to lessen the impact on and from nearby traffic. To avoid traffic and adapt to the physical terrain, LRT systems occasionally utilise abandoned railroad rights-of-way, underground tunnels, or even drainage canals. In the absence of separate crossings, LRT is granted transit signal priority to reduce delays and wait times at intersections.

LRT vehicles are powered by an overhead line. They can move and stop swiftly thanks to contemporary electronic propulsion and braking control. Although they can reach 66 mph with the newest technology, most LRT systems travel at an average speed of 10 to 30 mph.

Benefits or Features

  • larger capacity and speedier operation than other transit modes, such as light rail and bus rapid transit. Heavy rail travels at top speeds of up to 75 mph and may carry up to 2,400 passengers each train during rush hour.
  • provides passengers with a better riding experience, attracting additional riders from other modes. LRT is convenient, fast, silent, and secure. In order to make up for missed time in a car, passengers might opt to be productive or unwind.
  • provides more flexibility in terms of station sites and routes than other rail systems. Light rail can travel underground, on elevated platforms, over other railroad rights-of-way, or even in canal beds when necessary, even though it frequently shares the road with automobile traffic.
